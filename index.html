Why System 1/2 Cannot Plan

Structural Failures and Desiderata for Embodied Agents

Bingyang Kelvin Liu  ·  Ziyu Patrick Chen

Grainger College of Engineering, University of Illinois Urbana‑Champaign

Read the PDF Abstract Contributions Findings Footage Desiderata

Abstract

The embodied AI community has converged on dual-process (System 1/2) architectures, pairing fast reactive policies with slow deliberative planners. This paper is a position / problem-identification paper: we argue that this decomposition is architecturally incapable of genuine long-horizon planning—not as a quantitative shortfall, but as a structural one—and we characterize what any alternative architecture would have to look like, without proposing or validating a specific solution.

We identify three structural failure modes of the S1/S2 decomposition: the guidance bottleneck, reactive prior domination, and cross-scale binding failure. We demonstrate empirically that a state-of-the-art VA model drops from 98.0% to 36.0% instruction-following rate when instructions conflict with the reactive prior, and that composed out-of-distribution tasks yield a mean success rate of 24.7% against an in-distribution baseline exceeding 90%.

We then articulate four desiderata that any architecture aiming to escape these failure modes should satisfy: a single shared latent state, intrinsic multi-timescale temporal structure, continuous bidirectional coupling between motor dynamics and deliberative context, and a stability / attractor bias. We do not claim these desiderata are unique, minimal, or sufficient; establishing either property is beyond the scope of a single paper and is left as an open problem. We sketch one plausible direction—a shared-state dynamical architecture with structured temporal modes—as an illustration, not as a validated solution.

Three Structural Failure Modes

1. The Guidance Bottleneck

In current S1/S2 systems, the deliberative module issues a directive and the reactive module executes it without any feedback channel about motor-level state, execution difficulty, or plan feasibility. This one-way, low-bandwidth channel is the guidance interface. Three problems follow:

  • Motor feasibility is invisible to the planner. The planner cannot determine whether the reactive policy is capable of a compound behavior.
  • The deliberative–reactive boundary is not fixed. A two-system decomposition must assign each degree of freedom to one side a priori, yet competent behavior requires either.
  • Temporal coupling is bidirectional and continuous. Motor-level events must modulate deliberative priorities in real time, not via a perceive–abstract–re-plan loop at a slower timescale.

2. Reactive Prior Domination

Even when the System 2 module produces fine-grained instructions, the reactive prior of the System 1 module often overrides them. Systematic evaluations of video and action generation models reveal that even under explicit behavioral instructions, models still produce the most probable motion from their training distribution rather than following the input instruction.

Experiment 1 — Limb-instruction following. LingBot-VA is tested on 50 pick-and-place trials with explicit limb-arm instructions. When the instruction aligns with the model's default arm choice, the following rate is 49/50 (98.0%). When the instruction conflicts with the prior, the rate drops to 18/50 (36.0%).

3. Cross-Scale Binding Failure

A cross-scale binding task is any task in which the optimal low-level action depends on deliberative knowledge—a specific past event, a multi-step tactical plan, a context-dependent preference— that the guidance interface does not transmit at the required granularity.

Experiment 2 — Composed OOD tasks. Three composed pairs of distinct in-distribution behaviors were evaluated on 50 trials each:

  • handover_then_place_phone_stand — 28.0%
  • press_stapler_while_holding — 22.0%
  • open_laptop_then_place_object_inside — 24.0%

Mean: 24.7% — a significant degradation from the >90% in-distribution baseline.

Four Desiderata for What Comes Next

We refer informally to the class of architectures attempting to satisfy these desiderata as System Zero, to contrast with the S1/S2 framing. The name labels a direction, not a specific model.

D1 · Shared Representational Substrate

A candidate replacement should avoid producing reactive motor commands and deliberative plans through separate subsystems communicating only through a bandwidth-limited interface. What matters is whether deliberative context can influence motor selection without traversing a lossy commit point.

D2 · Temporal Structure That Doesn't Collapse Horizon onto Context Length

A candidate replacement should support long-horizon context through mechanisms whose cost scales more favorably—intrinsic recurrent dynamics, structured memory, hierarchical compression, or others— so horizon is not tied one-to-one to context-window tokens.

D3 · Continuous, Bidirectional Influence Between Motor Dynamics and Deliberative Context

A candidate replacement should admit continuous feedback from motor-level state into deliberative reasoning, and from deliberative context into motor selection, on timescales comparable to the reactive loop. Transient motor spikes should not corrupt the plan, but sustained motor patterns should be able to reshape it.

D4 · Goal-Directed Persistence Under Perturbation

A shared-substrate architecture without an inductive bias toward goal-consistent trajectories risks degenerating into an unconstrained recurrent memory that drifts under reactive pressure. Contractive dynamics, Lyapunov structure, energy-based landscapes, explicit goal anchoring—some mechanism that prevents such drift.

Status of D1–D4. These desiderata are diagnostic, not prescriptive. We conjecture they capture important structural requirements, but we offer no proof of necessity, sufficiency, or minimality.

How Future Work Could Test Candidates

We describe two benchmark families briefly; each could be developed into a full benchmark by subsequent work, with or without adopting our desiderata vocabulary.

Benchmark Family 1 · Cross-Scale Binding

Trials structured in three phases — a context phase that specifies a conditional behavioral policy, a delay phase with a filler task to prevent trivial working-memory retention, and an execution phase where the correct response depends on the earlier cue. Useful metrics: conditional accuracy, context-decay curves over delay length, and a score isolating trials where the correct response contradicts the reactive prior.

Benchmark Family 2 · Representational Probes

A natural approach is to measure mutual information between substates of the model (slow vs. fast components, early vs. late layers, learned hierarchies) and task-relevant reference signals (current subgoal, ground-truth action, reactive event indicators) using established MI estimators.

Limitations

No solution, no validation.
We do not implement, train, or evaluate any candidate replacement architecture.
Desiderata are diagnostic, not proven.
We offer D1–D4 as starting vocabulary for evaluating candidate architectures, not as theorems.
Empirical scope.
The 98%→36% drop and 24.7% composed-OOD rate are limited to a single VA-model family on RoboTwin 2.0.
Analytical vs. causal claims.
Our analysis is analytical: we argue that failure modes follow from the decomposition, not that no interface engineering could ever mask them.
